Guidelines

These guidelines are adapted from open-source and authoritative public sources, including WHO Guiding Principles on Human Cell, Tissue and Organ Transplantation (2010) and principles aligned with national bodies such as NOTTO (National Organ and Tissue Transplant Organisation). They are for general awareness only; always follow your country’s laws and hospital policies.

Ethical principles

Organ donation and transplantation must be guided by respect for human dignity, autonomy, and solidarity. The following principles form the foundation of ethical practice:

  • Voluntary, non-remunerated donation — Donation from both deceased and living donors should be voluntary and without financial gain. No payment or reward should be offered or received for organs.
  • No organ trafficking or transplant tourism — Financial gain from organ transactions is opposed; systems must prevent trafficking and commercialisation. Patients and donors must be protected from exploitation.
  • Protection of vulnerable people — Safeguards must protect potential donors and recipients from coercion, abuse, or exploitation. Special care applies to minors and other vulnerable groups.
  • Transparent, equitable allocation — Allocation should be guided by clinical criteria and ethical norms, not by wealth, privilege, or geography. Fair access is a core goal.
  • Long-term follow-up for living donors — Living donors should receive appropriate medical care and follow-up before, during, and after donation.

Donation must always be voluntary and non-remunerated; allocation must be transparent and equitable. These are non-negotiable ethical standards.

Source: WHO Guiding Principles on Human Cell, Tissue and Organ Transplantation (Resolution WHA63.22, 2010).

Donor eligibility (general)

Eligibility depends on the type of donation and on national and hospital-level criteria. The following is a general framework:

Deceased donation: Cells, tissues and organs may be removed from deceased persons for transplantation when any legally required consent has been obtained (e.g. from the donor during life or from the family after death) and there is no reason to believe the deceased objected to donation. Medical suitability is assessed by the transplant team.

Consent is mandatory for deceased donation—either from the donor's prior registration or from the family or legal authority as per local law.

Living donation: Living donation is permitted with emphasis on donor health, informed consent, and protection. The donor must be medically and psychologically evaluated. Final eligibility is always determined by the treating medical team and national regulations. Living donors must be able to withdraw consent without penalty.

Adapted from WHO Guiding Principles; local laws and hospital criteria apply.

Process (registration and consent)

LifeLink follows a clear process to ensure consent and data are handled correctly:

  1. Donors register through the official consent form and provide accurate personal and health information (including conditions, medications, allergies, and relevant history).
  2. Consent must be informed, voluntary, and documented. Donors should understand the purpose of donation and how their data will be used. Withdrawal of consent should be possible as per local policy.
  3. Donor data is stored and shared only for the purpose of organ matching and in line with data protection laws. No use for commercial or unauthorised purposes is permitted.
  4. Access to full donor details and availability is restricted to authorised hospitals, not the general public. This protects donor privacy and ensures professional use of the information.
  5. At the time of actual donation, medical teams confirm eligibility and consent again according to national and hospital protocols. LifeLink supports the process; it does not replace medical or legal verification.

Consent must be informed and voluntary; access to donor details is restricted to authorised hospitals only. These rules protect both donors and the integrity of the system.

Legal and institutional oversight

Transplantation activities should be subject to national oversight and coordination. Regulatory bodies (such as NOTTO in India or equivalent authorities elsewhere) set standards, monitor safety, and ensure compliance. Safety, efficacy, adverse event reporting, and international cooperation to prevent trafficking are part of the WHO framework and of many national laws.

LifeLink aligns with these principles by restricting sensitive donor information to hospitals and by using consent-based registration—so that the platform supports, rather than bypasses, legal and ethical oversight.

Hospitals using LifeLink are expected to comply with their national laws and institutional policies. When in doubt, consult your transplant coordination office or national authority.
